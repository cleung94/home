What I Learned From Being Laid Off

Two weeks. That was the time I was given before my role disappeared. At the time it felt shocking — but it became a moment that reshaped how I think about work and purpose.

March 19, 2026 5 min read

I still remember the moment the conversation started.

We were standing in the middle of the parking lot, and I could tell how stressful the situation was when he shakily brought out a pack of cigarettes.

"Your assignment here at Meta is ending in two weeks."

I didn’t react in the moment. For a second, time froze.

I didn’t have time to think, and I didn’t really have the space to react. Everything felt surreal, but I had to cling to reality. What do I need to do before I leave? What about the projects I’ve been assigned and still need to close out? Who am I going to transfer my responsibilities to?

I needed to figure out what to do with the work I had spent nine years of my life building, curating, and caring for.

The day passed by, and eventually the numbness started to subside.

Questions started forming immediately:

What happened?

What did I do wrong?

Was my performance poor?

What comes next?

Even when you understand that layoffs are often tied to larger organizational decisions, it still feels personal. Especially with a short timeline, it creates a strange mix of emotions.

Shock.

Uncertainty.

Reflection.

When your peers around you are questioning why you are being let go—when they remind you of your importance and how central your work had become to so many processes—it still doesn’t feel real.

The Quiet Reflection

It was time to move on.

After spending so many years at Meta, I had grown comfortable. I was proud of the work I had done to streamline processes, strengthen operations, and reinforce them as part of my daily responsibilities. That had become part of who I was. I had built and shaped many things during my time there, and now it was time to leave that legacy behind and make room for something new.

That moment created space for something I hadn’t really allowed myself to stop and think about before:

What kind of work actually felt meaningful to me?

During my time at Meta, I started to realize that something felt incomplete in the rhythm of routine life.

Wake up. Go to work. Come home. Eat dinner. Play games. Go to sleep.

I wanted more meaningful moments in my life, so I started volunteering at care homes and community projects. That eventually led me to pursue my Certified Nursing Assistant license. I found myself working two jobs—one as a priority, and the other as a source of fulfillment. I realized I had been missing something.

What I needed was not just to continue along a career path, but to move toward something that felt more meaningful and purposeful. In a strange way, I needed the layoff—and that moment of clarity—to help push me toward something new.

Looking Back

Looking back now, that two-week notice still feels surreal.

At the time, it felt like the ground had shifted beneath my feet. But with distance, I can see it differently now. I will miss my peers and coworkers deeply. The memories and moments we shared together are something I will always hold in my heart and mind.

But sometimes, the moments that feel like endings are actually the beginnings of something new.

And sometimes, the most important career decisions come from the moments we never expected to face.

It reminds me of a quote from Meet the Robinsons:

“Around here, however, we don’t look backward for very long. We keep moving forward, opening up new doors and doing new things, and curiosity keeps leading us down new paths."
